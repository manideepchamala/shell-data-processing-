	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Cricket ball</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Ball used to play cricket</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A_Cricket_ball.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/A_Cricket_ball.jpg/220px-A_Cricket_ball.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/A_Cricket_ball.jpg/330px-A_Cricket_ball.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/A_Cricket_ball.jpg/440px-A_Cricket_ball.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A_Cricket_ball.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A cricket ball</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>cricket ball</b> is a hard, solid <a href="/wiki/Ball" title="Ball">ball</a> used to play <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a>. A cricket ball consists of a <a href="/wiki/Cork_(material)" title="Cork (material)">cork</a> core wound with <a href="/wiki/String_(structure)" title="String (structure)">string</a> then a <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> cover stitched on, and manufacture is regulated by cricket law at <a href="/wiki/First-class_cricket" title="First-class cricket">first-class</a> level. The trajectory of a cricket ball when bowled, through movement in the air, and off the ground, is influenced by the action of the bowler and the condition of the ball and the pitch, while working on the cricket ball to obtain optimum condition is a key role of the fielding side. The principal method through which the <a href="/wiki/Batsman_(cricket)" class="mw-redirect" title="Batsman (cricket)">batsman</a> scores <a href="/wiki/Run_(cricket)" title="Run (cricket)">runs</a> is by hitting the ball, with the bat, into a position where it would be safe to take a run, or by directing the ball through or over the <a href="/wiki/Boundary_(cricket)" title="Boundary (cricket)">boundary</a>. Cricket balls are harder and heavier than <a href="/wiki/Baseball_(ball)" title="Baseball (ball)">baseballs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In day <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test cricket</a>, professional domestic games that spread over a multitude of days, and almost the entirety of amateur cricket, the traditional red cricket ball is normally used. In many <a href="/wiki/One_day_cricket" class="mw-redirect" title="One day cricket">one day cricket</a> matches, a white ball is used instead in order to remain visible under floodlights, and since 2010, pink has been introduced to contrast with players' white clothing and for improved night visibility during day/night Test matches.<sup id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Training balls of white, red and pink are also common, and <a href="/wiki/Tennis_ball" title="Tennis ball">tennis balls</a> and other similar-sized balls can be used for training or informal cricket matches. During cricket matches, the quality of the ball changes to a point where it is no longer usable, and during this decline its properties alter and thus can influence the match. Altering the state of the cricket ball outside the permitted manners designated in the rules of cricket is prohibited during a match, and so-called "<a href="/wiki/Ball_tampering" title="Ball tampering">ball tampering</a>" has resulted in numerous controversies.
</p><p>Injuries and <a href="/wiki/List_of_fatal_accidents_in_cricket" class="mw-redirect" title="List of fatal accidents in cricket">fatalities</a> have been caused by cricket balls during matches.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The hazards posed by cricket balls were a key motivator for the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Cricket_equipment" class="mw-redirect" title="Cricket equipment">protective equipment</a>. 
<b></b>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacture">Manufacture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cricket_ball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Manufacture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/British_standards" class="mw-redirect" title="British standards">British Standard</a> <a href="/wiki/BS_5993" title="BS 5993">BS 5993</a> specifies the construction details, dimensions, quality and performance of cricket balls.
</p><p>A cricket ball is made with a core of <a href="/wiki/Cork_(material)" title="Cork (material)">cork</a>, which is layered with tightly wound string, and covered by a leather case with a slightly raised sewn seam. In a top-quality ball suitable for the highest levels of competition, the covering is constructed of four pieces of leather shaped similar to the peel of a quartered orange, but one hemisphere is rotated by 90 degrees with respect to the other. The "equator" of the ball is stitched with string to form the ball's prominent seam, with six rows of stitches. The remaining two joins between the leather pieces are stitched internally forming the <a href="/wiki/Quarter_seam" title="Quarter seam">quarter seam</a>. Lower-quality balls with a two-piece covering are also popular for practice and lower-level competition due to their lower cost.
</p>

<p>The nature of the cricket ball slightly varies with its manufacturer. White <a href="/wiki/Kookaburra_Sport" title="Kookaburra Sport">Kookaburra balls</a> are used in one-day  and <a href="/wiki/Twenty20" title="Twenty20">Twenty20</a> international matches, while red Kookaburras are used in test matches played in most of the ten test-playing nations,<sup id="cite_ref-ABC_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> except for the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies_cricket_team" title="West Indies cricket team">West Indies</a> and <a href="/wiki/England_cricket_team" title="England cricket team">England</a>, who use <a href="/wiki/British_Cricket_Balls_Ltd" title="British Cricket Balls Ltd">Dukes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India_national_cricket_team" title="India national cricket team">India</a>, who use <a href="/wiki/Sanspareils_Greenlands" title="Sanspareils Greenlands">SG</a> balls.<sup id="cite_ref-Dailytimes_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dailytimes-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cricket_ball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colour">Colour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cricket_ball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Colour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:White_ball_2.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/White_ball_2.JPG/220px-White_ball_2.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/White_ball_2.JPG/330px-White_ball_2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/White_ball_2.JPG/440px-White_ball_2.JPG 2x" data-file-width="964" data-file-height="719" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:White_ball_2.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>White balls are used in many limited overs cricket matches, especially those involving floodlights (day/night games). This is because a red ball under yellow floodlights takes on a brownish colour which is very similar to the colour of the pitch.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A_used_pink_ball_at_the_2014_English_county_season_launch_in_UAE.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/A_used_pink_ball_at_the_2014_English_county_season_launch_in_UAE.JPG/220px-A_used_pink_ball_at_the_2014_English_county_season_launch_in_UAE.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="207" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/A_used_pink_ball_at_the_2014_English_county_season_launch_in_UAE.JPG/330px-A_used_pink_ball_at_the_2014_English_county_season_launch_in_UAE.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/A_used_pink_ball_at_the_2014_English_county_season_launch_in_UAE.JPG/440px-A_used_pink_ball_at_the_2014_English_county_season_launch_in_UAE.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3271" data-file-height="3083" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A_used_pink_ball_at_the_2014_English_county_season_launch_in_UAE.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A pink cricket ball. Pink balls deteriorate more slowly than white balls, but have better night visibility than red balls, making them the most suitable ball for day-night Test cricket.<sup id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Cricket balls are traditionally red, and red balls are used in <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test cricket</a> and <a href="/wiki/First-class_cricket" title="First-class cricket">First-class cricket</a> but proposals to introduce other colours date back at least as early as 1937.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>White balls were introduced when <a href="/wiki/One-day_cricket" class="mw-redirect" title="One-day cricket">one-day matches</a> began being played at night under floodlights, as they are more visible at night; all professional one-day matches are now played with white balls, even when they are not played at night. The white balls have been found to behave differently from the red balls: most notably, they swing a lot more during the first half of an innings than the red balls, and they deteriorate more quickly. Manufacturers claim that white and red balls are manufactured using the same methods and materials,<sup id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> other than the dyeing of the leather. Another problem associated with white cricket balls used in <a href="/wiki/One_Day_Internationals" class="mw-redirect" title="One Day Internationals">One Day Internationals</a> is that they quickly become dirty or dull in colour, which makes it more difficult for batsmen to sight the ball after 30–40 overs of use.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Since October 2012, this has been managed by the use of two new white balls in each innings, with a different ball used from each bowling end; the same strategy was used in the <a href="/wiki/1992_Cricket_World_Cup" title="1992 Cricket World Cup">1992</a> and <a href="/wiki/1996_Cricket_World_Cup" title="1996 Cricket World Cup">1996</a> Cricket World Cups. Between October 2007 and October 2012, the issue had been managed using one new ball from the start of the innings, then swapping it at the end of the 34th over with a "reconditioned ball", which was neither new nor too dirty to see. Before October 2007, except during 1992 and 1996 World Cups, only one ball was used during an innings of an ODI and it was at the umpires' discretion to change the ball if it was difficult to see.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pink balls were developed in the <a href="/wiki/2000s_(decade)" title="2000s (decade)">2000s</a> to enable Tests and first-class matches played at night. The red ball is unsuited to night tests due to poor visibility, and the white ball is unsuited to first-class cricket because it deteriorates rapidly and cannot be used for eighty overs as specified in the rules, so the pink ball was designed to provide a satisfactory compromise on both issues. It is still considered more difficult to see than a white ball; and the leather is more heavily dyed than a red ball, which better preserves its colour and visibility as it wears but also gives it slightly different wear characteristics. It has performed well enough in testing and first-class cricket to be approved for use in international cricket.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> A pink ball was used for the first time in an international match in July 2009 when the England Women's team defeated Australia in a one-day match at Wormsley,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and a pink ball was used in a day-night Test match for the first time in November 2015. Other colours were also experimented with, such as yellow and orange (glowing composite), for improved night visibility, but pink proved to be the preferred option.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_status">Current status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cricket_ball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2014, the ball used in Test match cricket in England had a UK recommended retail price of £100.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Test_match_cricket" class="mw-redirect" title="Test match cricket">test match cricket</a> this ball is used for a minimum of 80 overs (theoretically five hours and twenty minutes of play), after which the fielding side has the option of using a new ball. In professional one day cricket, at least two new balls are used for each match. Amateur cricketers often have to use old balls, or cheap substitutes, in which case the changes in the condition of the ball may be different from professional cricket.
</p><p>There are three main manufacturers of cricket balls used in international matches: <a href="/wiki/Kookaburra_Sport" title="Kookaburra Sport">Kookaburra</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Cricket_Balls_Ltd" title="British Cricket Balls Ltd">Dukes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanspareils_Greenlands" title="Sanspareils Greenlands">SG</a>. The manufacturer of the red (or pink) balls used for Tests varies depending on location: India uses SG; England, Ireland and the West Indies use Dukes; and all other countries use Kookaburra. The different manufacturers' balls behave differently: for instance Dukes balls have a prouder seam and will tend to swing more than Kookaburra balls<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> – providing a home advantage when playing against a team unfamiliar with the ball. All limited overs international matches, regardless of location, are played with white Kookaburra balls.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> White Dukes balls were used at the <a href="/wiki/1999_Cricket_World_Cup" title="1999 Cricket World Cup">1999 Cricket World Cup</a>, but the ball behaved more erratically than the Kookaburra and white Dukes have not been used since. Domestic competitions may use a domestic manufacturer: for example, Pakistan uses Grays balls in its first-class competitions.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cricket balls can be bowled at close to 160&#160;km/h (100&#160;mph) by pace bowlers and made to deviate from a straight course, both in the air (known as 'swinging') and off the ground (known as 'seaming'). A spin bowler imparts lateral revolutions on the ball at the point of delivery, so that when it bounces it deviates from a straight course. As cricket bats have become thicker, the ball can now be hit well over 100 metres before touching the ground.
</p><p>Cricket commentator and former Test bowler <a href="/wiki/Simon_Doull" title="Simon Doull">Simon Doull</a> noted that cricket balls produced after <a href="/wiki/2015_Cricket_World_Cup" title="2015 Cricket World Cup">Cricket World Cup 2015</a> produced a lot less swing regardless of manufacturer. This was said to be apparent in <a href="/wiki/2017_ICC_Champions_Trophy" title="2017 ICC Champions Trophy">2017 ICC Champions Trophy</a>, even on traditionally swing-friendly British pitches, particularly with white balls, but the former West Indian bowler <a href="/wiki/Ian_Bishop_(cricketer,_born_1967)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ian Bishop (cricketer, born 1967)">Ian Bishop</a> was unwilling to support this.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Condition_of_a_cricket_ball">Condition of a cricket ball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cricket_ball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Condition of a cricket ball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cricketball.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Cricketball.png/220px-Cricketball.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b0/Cricketball.png 1.5x" data-file-width="325" data-file-height="325" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cricketball.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A new cricket ball</div></div></div>
<p>In Test cricket, a new ball is used at the start of each <a href="/wiki/Innings" title="Innings">innings</a> in a match. In Limited Over Internationals, two new balls, one from each end, are used at the start of each innings. A cricket ball may not be replaced except under specific conditions described in the <i><a href="/wiki/Laws_of_Cricket" title="Laws of Cricket">Laws of Cricket</a></i>:
</p>
<ul><li>If the ball becomes damaged or lost.</li>
<li>If the condition of the ball is illegally modified by a player.</li>
<li>In Test cricket, after 80 <a href="/wiki/Over_(cricket)" title="Over (cricket)">overs</a>, the captain of the bowling side has the option to take a new ball.</li></ul>
<p>The ball is not replaced if it is hit into the crowd - the crowd must return it. If the ball is damaged, lost, or illegally modified, it will be replaced by a <i>used</i> ball in similar condition to the replaced ball. A <i>new</i> ball can only be used after the specified minimum number of overs have been bowled with the old one.
</p><p>Because a single ball is used for an extended period of play, its surface wears down and becomes rough. The <a href="/wiki/Bowler_(cricket)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bowler (cricket)">bowlers</a> may polish it whenever they can, usually by rubbing it on their trousers, producing the characteristic red stain that can often be seen there. However, they will usually only polish one side of the ball, in order to create 'swing' as it travels through the air. They may apply saliva or sweat to the ball as they polish it.
</p><p>The seam of a cricket ball can also be used to produce different trajectories through the air, with the technique known as <a href="/wiki/Swing_bowling" title="Swing bowling">swing bowling</a>, or to produce sideways movement as it bounces off the <a href="/wiki/Cricket_pitch" title="Cricket pitch">pitch</a>, with the technique known as <a href="/wiki/Seam_bowling" title="Seam bowling">seam bowling</a>.
</p><p>Since the condition of the cricket ball is crucial to the amount of movement through the air a bowler can produce, the laws governing what players may and may not do to the ball are specific and rigorously enforced. The <a href="/wiki/Umpire_(cricket)" title="Umpire (cricket)">umpires</a> will inspect the ball frequently during a match. If the ball is out of shape due to normal wear and tear due to batting and ball hitting the pitch, a ball of similar usage rate and condition will be replaced: e.g. a ball about 30 overs old will be replaced by a ball about the same age.
</p><p>It is illegal for a player to:
</p>
<ul><li>rub any substance apart from saliva or sweat onto the ball</li>
<li>rub the ball on the ground</li>
<li>scuff the ball with any rough object, including the fingernails</li>
<li>pick at or lift the seam of the ball.</li></ul>
<p>Despite these rules, it can be tempting for players to gain an advantage by breaking them. There have been a handful of incidents of so-called <a href="/wiki/Ball_tampering" title="Ball tampering">ball tampering</a> at the highest levels of cricket.
</p><p>A new cricket ball is harder than a worn one, and is preferred by <a href="/wiki/Fast_bowling" class="mw-redirect" title="Fast bowling">fast bowlers</a> because of the speed and bounce of the ball off the pitch. Older balls tend to spin more as the roughness grips the pitch more when the ball bounces, so <a href="/wiki/Spin_bowling" title="Spin bowling">spin bowlers</a> prefer to use a worn ball, though a ball of about 8–10 overs old is still useful to a spinner as it can get more drift in the air. Uneven wear on older balls may also make <a href="/wiki/Reverse_swing" class="mw-redirect" title="Reverse swing">reverse swing</a> possible. A captain may delay the request for a new ball if she/he prefers to have spin bowlers operating, but usually asks for the new ball soon after it becomes available.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dangers_of_cricket_balls">Dangers of cricket balls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cricket_ball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dangers of cricket balls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_fatal_accidents_in_cricket" class="mw-redirect" title="List of fatal accidents in cricket">List of fatal accidents in cricket</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Used_cricket_ball.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Used_cricket_ball.jpg/220px-Used_cricket_ball.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Used_cricket_ball.jpg/330px-Used_cricket_ball.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Used_cricket_ball.jpg/440px-Used_cricket_ball.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2268" data-file-height="1512" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Used_cricket_ball.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A used cricket ball</div></div></div>
<p>Cricket balls are hard and potentially lethal, so most of today's batsmen and close fielders often wear <a href="/wiki/Cricket_equipment" class="mw-redirect" title="Cricket equipment">protective equipment</a>. Cricket ball injuries are fairly frequent, including eye (with some players having lost eyes),<sup id="cite_ref-bjsm-criceye_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bjsm-criceye-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> head and face,<sup id="cite_ref-espn-ump_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-espn-ump-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> finger and toe,<sup id="cite_ref-spinj-cric_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spinj-cric-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> teeth<sup id="cite_ref-pdc-teeth_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pdc-teeth-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and testicular injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-spinj-cric_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spinj-cric-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Frederick,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Frederick, Prince of Wales">Frederick, Prince of Wales</a> (1707–1751) is often said to have died of complications after being hit by a cricket ball, although the connection between the incident and his actual cause of death is unproven. <a href="/wiki/Glamorgan_County_Cricket_Club" title="Glamorgan County Cricket Club">Glamorgan</a> player <a href="/wiki/Roger_Davis_(cricketer)" title="Roger Davis (cricketer)">Roger Davis</a> was seriously injured by a ball in 1971 when he was hit on the head while fielding.<sup id="cite_ref-cricinfo-davis_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cricinfo-davis-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The Indian batsman <a href="/wiki/Nari_Contractor" title="Nari Contractor">Nariman 'Nari' Contractor</a> had to retire from the game after being hit on the head by a ball in the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a> in 1962.<sup id="cite_ref-mid-nari_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mid-nari-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1998, Indian cricketer <a href="/wiki/Raman_Lamba" title="Raman Lamba">Raman Lamba</a> died when a cricket ball hit his head in a club match in <a href="/wiki/Dhaka" title="Dhaka">Dhaka</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cricinfo-lambda_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cricinfo-lambda-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Lamba was fielding at forward short leg without a helmet when a ball struck by batsman <a href="/wiki/Mehrab_Hossain_(cricketer,_born_1978)" title="Mehrab Hossain (cricketer, born 1978)">Mehrab Hossain</a> hit him hard on the head and rebounded to wicket-keeper <a href="/wiki/Khaled_Mashud" title="Khaled Mashud">Khaled Mashud</a>.
</p><p>A cricket umpire, Alcwyn Jenkins, died in 2009 in <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> after being hit on the head by a ball thrown by a fielder.<sup id="cite_ref-espn-ump_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-espn-ump-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 27 October 2013, South African cricketer <a href="/wiki/Darryn_Randall" title="Darryn Randall">Darryn Randall</a> died after being hit on the head by the ball while batting. He collapsed immediately and was rushed to the <a href="/wiki/Alice,_Eastern_Cape" title="Alice, Eastern Cape">Alice</a> Hospital, but the medical staff could not revive him.
</p><p>In November 2014, Australia and South Australia batsman <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Hughes" title="Phillip Hughes">Phillip Hughes</a> died at the age of 25 at a Sydney hospital after he was hit on the side of the neck by a <a href="/wiki/Bouncer_(cricket)" title="Bouncer (cricket)">bouncer</a> bowled by <a href="/wiki/Sean_Abbott" title="Sean Abbott">Sean Abbott</a> during a <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Shield" title="Sheffield Shield">Sheffield Shield</a> game.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The same week, <a href="/wiki/Hillel_Oscar" class="mw-redirect" title="Hillel Oscar">Hillel Oscar</a>, an umpire and former captain of Israel's national cricket team, died after being hit in the neck by a ball.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 14 August 2017, Zubair Ahmed died after being hit on the head while batting in a club match played in the <a href="/wiki/Mardan_District" title="Mardan District">Mardan District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khyber_Pakhtunkhwa" title="Khyber Pakhtunkhwa">Khyber Pakhtunkhwa</a>, Pakistan.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_to_cricket_balls">Alternatives to cricket balls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cricket_ball&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Alternatives to cricket balls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ball_3.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Ball_3.JPG/220px-Ball_3.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Ball_3.JPG/330px-Ball_3.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Ball_3.JPG/440px-Ball_3.JPG 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ball_3.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A yellow alternative cricket ball</div></div></div>
<p>Sometimes alternatives to a real cricket ball may be preferred for reasons of safety, availability and cost. Examples include a tennis ball and a plastic version of the cricket ball.
</p><p>Many casual players use a <a href="/wiki/Tennis_ball" title="Tennis ball">tennis ball</a> wrapped in layers of some type of <a href="/wiki/Adhesive_tape" title="Adhesive tape">adhesive tape</a> (often electrical tape), which makes the relatively soft tennis ball harder and smoother. This is commonly referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Tape_ball" title="Tape ball">tape ball</a>. A common variant is to tape only half the tennis ball, to provide two different sides and make it easy to bowl with prodigious amounts of <a href="/wiki/Swing_bowling" title="Swing bowling">swing</a>.
</p><p>Younger players often use either tennis balls or an air-filled plastic 'windball' for safety reasons before using the 'hard' cricket ball after a certain age: <a href="/wiki/Windball_cricket" title="Windball cricket">windball cricket</a> is also a popular sport in its own right. They might also use an 'IncrediBall' or an 'Aeroball' whilst making the step between windballs and 'hard' cricket balls. These balls are designed to mimic the feel, speed and bounce of a regular hard ball, but soften when coming in contact with objects at high speed, reducing the risk of injury.
</p>
